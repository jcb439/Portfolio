AirBend

Passive Cooling Wearable

Course: DesignTech Studio (Spring 2025)

Collaborators: Anxin Lian, Vita Sipayung

AirBend addresses the critical challenge of thermal discomfort faced by users in hot environments made increasingly hotter by global warming. Daily heat exposure is becoming an extremely important global issue, yet most cooling wearables are powered, rigid, and not built for everyday movement.

There's a need for a passive and flexible solution that cools the body through natural motion. Paired with temperature sensors and a digital app, the AirBend system makes temperature awareness personal, adaptive, and effortless.

How might we utilize the body's natural bending & motion to passively ventilate users when they are too hot?

Precedents

Passive Cooling Systems

The Tangible Media Group's bioLogic project uses living Bacillus Subtilis Natto cells as nanoactuators that expand and contract in response to atmospheric moisture. Their "Second Skin" garment features bio-hybrid film that reacts to body heat and sweat, causing flaps around heat zones to open for natural ventilation. (bioLogic)

Kirigami Principles

The ancient art of kirigami—paper cutting that creates 3D forms from 2D surfaces—provided the foundation for our pattern development. Strategic cuts allow flat materials to expand and transform through tension. (Corrigan et al.)

System Overview

Click to view full screen

AirBend integrates three core methods to maximize user cooling by optimizing shape-changing cut geometries on a wearable. The design process combines geometry exploration to develop kirigami-inspired cut patterns, human form mapping to align openings with high-sweat zones, and material and technology testing to evaluate fabric behavior and sensor integration.

Our optimization objective was to maximize the total opening area created by cuts while ensuring the wearable conforms to the user's 3D body geometry, following body contours without causing discomfort. Opening geometries were prioritized across body regions most prone to overheating, based on empirical body heat index data.

How the System Works

The system works by first having users input their health and lifestyle information into the companion app—creating a profile with activity patterns, demographics, location, and health conditions. Based on this data, a personalized temperature threshold is established for each user. Embedded sensors in the wearable actively track both body temperature and surrounding air temperature in real-time. When the sensors detect data exceeding the user's personal threshold, they send a push notification alerting the user to perform specific stretching motions. These bending movements passively activate the kirigami-patterned cuts in the wearable, opening ventilation channels that allow air to flow through and cool the body.

Method 1: Geometry Exploration

Kirigami Pattern Exploration

We began by exploring four distinct kirigami-inspired patterns, evaluating each across multiple criteria: texture on skin, opening pattern clarity, actuation ease/force, and suitability for different body regions. Each pattern was scored based on these objective factors to determine which design would be most effective for passive body-cooling through natural movement. The winning pattern from this evaluation became the foundation for our subsequent sampling and filtering process.

Physical Study A: Initial Sampling

We laser-cut five combinations of geometric variables (T, W, L, H) on paper to evaluate performance. Using the MulticolorEngine API, we quantified the percentage of surface opening in each configuration with color-coded overlays—separating cut area (orange) from static surface (purple). Constraints included surface area boundaries based on torso measurements and minimum cut spacing to maintain structural integrity. This revealed a positive correlation between length (L) and height (H) values and total opening area.

Physical Study B: Optimization

Building on initial findings, we extended L and H values to refine the design for maximal airflow while ensuring material durability. From the first round, we established minimum threshold values necessary for effective actuation: T = 1.7 cm, H = 0.85 cm, W = 0.3 cm, L = 0.7 cm. We then tested larger values and observed fabric behavior under stretch. The optimal configuration was identified as: T = 1.2 cm, H = 0.95 cm, W = 0.4 cm, L = 1.2 cm, achieving up to 19.9% opening area when stretched. Values larger than these were found unsuitable for the project goals.

Method 2: Human Form

Sweat Zone Exploration

We integrated empirical data from upper body sweat maps to guide kirigami cut placement and pattern scaling. Higher sweating zones—along the middle of the chest and across the back—were prioritized for larger opening scaling. Lower sweating zones received smaller or fewer cuts to balance ventilation with structural coverage. (Dong et al.)

Movement & Stretch Mapping

We analyzed how the torso moves during common activities—walking, reaching, bending—to identify areas of maximum fabric stretch. These movement zones were cross-referenced with sweat zones to determine optimal cut placement that would actuate naturally during daily motion.

Applying Pattern to Bodysuit

Using Grasshopper, we established a standard bodysuit geometry and assigned zones correlating to body heat maps. The optimized "winning" pattern was standardized based on a basic unit that could be imported and scaled based on specified zone geometries. The kirigami cut geometries were scaled across sweating zones and morphed to accommodate the 3D curvature of the human body. This pattern-morphing ensured cuts lay comfortably on the user—certain zones are aligned off from 90-degree orientations due to the bodysuit's curves and the objective to conform to body contours.

Method 3: Material & Technology

Temperature Sensor Testing

We tested LilyPad Arduino sewable electronics with embedded temperature sensors to enable real-time heat monitoring. Multiple sensor placements were evaluated to capture body temperature variations across sweat zones. The sensors connect to the mobile app via Bluetooth, feeding personalized heat data to the user interface.

Material Testing

We conducted CNC laser cutting tests on two poly-blend fabrics: 85% polyester/15% spandex (blue) and 80% polyester/20% spandex (black). The blue polyblend was selected for the final prototype because it provided the desired stretch, was not see-through, maintained structural integrity, and had better recovery after repeated stretching.

Integrated App Interface

The companion mobile app collects user information (age, health conditions, activity levels) to determine heat sensitivity and set customized alerts. Sign-up pages establish individual temperature thresholds, the profile page stores health data, the monitoring dashboard provides real-time temperature tracking, and the notification system triggers alerts using forced vibration and sends warnings to both users and linked caregiver accounts when extreme heat is detected.

Final Prototype Fabrication

The final 1:1 scale bodysuit pattern was laser-cut using CNC machines in the College of Human Ecology on a stretchy and lightweight polyblend material. The blue fabric (85% polyester/15% spandex) was selected because it provided the desired stretch, was not see-through, maintained structural integrity, and had better recovery after repeated stretching compared to the black alternative (80% polyester/20% spandex). We sewed the pieces together using a simple straight line stitch along the top and side seams, leaving the armholes and bottom open. This method maintained the fabric's stretch and flexibility while preserving the integrity of the kirigami cut design.

Final Prototype

The final prototype demonstrates our passive cooling wearable in action. Through iterative physical studies and computational mapping, AirBend integrates kirigami-inspired cut patterns that open in response to natural body movement during exercise, providing ventilation exactly when and where users need it most.

Future Explorations

While our final prototype demonstrated promising optimization results, limited time for physical fabric prototyping leaves room for future improvements in design development and fabric testing to enhance user comfort and bending activation. Future work would involve testing our optimized cut pattern across a variety of stretchy and breathable fabrics using CNC fabric cutting to validate these findings. Designing for multiple body sizes would enable personalized optimization of bodysuit patterns to accommodate a broader range of users. Real-world wear testing across different climates would further validate our proof-of-concept for passive bending activation and maximized user cooling.
